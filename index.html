Who Am I?

AAs a child, I had two consistent passions. I loved learning about computers and I loved learning about the brain. My parents would always joke that even at the age of 9, I was the one to set up all the technology around the house and knew intuitively how to operate any devices around the house. Simultaneously, I was the child who borrowed my parent’s medical school textbooks to learn more about human anatomy and the brain. Growing up, I assumed these two passions were parallel and not be intersected, so at the start of college I opted for a degree in Psychology focusing in Biological and Evolutionary Sciences and minors in English and Sociology so I could approach the brain and cognitive sciences from all angles: biologically, philosophically, anthropologically, linguistically, and psychologically. I loved college and was so deeply entrenched in my learning that I worked for two different research labs to better understand our wonderful human minds. During my senior year, reading works of Piaget pivoted my interest to consider the human brain over time and I became fascinated with child development both physically and academically. Joining Teach for America (TFA), I had the chance to better understand first hand how humans learn and grow over time.

During all this, my love for technology never faded. In my research labs, I was notorious for being the master of excel and documentation. In TFA, I was known as the teacher who tracked data meticulously and used cutting edge EdTech in the classroom for student engagement. It was no surprise to anyone when I was asked to teach Computer Science alongside of Math and Science in Boston. Having no prior knowledge in coding, the idea of learning to code and teaching code was exhilarating. I love to learn and I am a huge self-learner, so as soon as I accepted the position, I begin to teach myself how to code. I became so involved with computer science I was soon a leader of the school’s Robotics team and founded Girls Who Code at my school.

It was in the middle of teaching coding that I realized that my two passions in life-- technology and cognitive sciences-- were not parallels but in fact very much an aligned industry in 2018. It is now with great enthusiasm that I pursue the exact alignment of my life-long passions: I seek to be a Full Stack Developer in Boston for a company at the fringes of innovation intersecting at cognitive science and technology.
